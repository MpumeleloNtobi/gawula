# Customer Workshop: Food Delivery Feature Discovery

## Objective

Run real customer discovery with Uber Eats and Mr D users to learn what food delivery features or functionality they would value most, then determine whether Gawula should prioritize multi-store ordering, meal completion, group ordering, trust improvements, or another wedge.

This workshop must not lead with multi-store ordering. The feature should earn its place from real customer stories.

## Primary Research Questions

1. What do frequent food delivery users wish Uber Eats or Mr D let them do?
2. What ordering moments create compromise, anxiety, extra fees, or support pain?
3. Do customers naturally describe a need to complete one meal across different stores, categories, or preferences?
4. Which features would make customers try Gawula instead of Uber Eats or Mr D?
5. What trust conditions must be true before customers accept one order across multiple stores?

## Participant Segments

Run three 90-minute sessions with 6-8 participants each.

1. Solo frequent users: order food delivery at least 3 times per month for themselves.
2. Household orderers: order for partners, families, roommates, or children.
3. Group orderers: coordinate orders for office teams, students, friends, or social groups.

## Recruit Criteria

Participants must:

1. Be 18 or older.
2. Live in a current or likely Gawula launch area.
3. Have used Uber Eats or Mr D at least 3 times in the last 30 days.
4. Have ordered for at least one shared meal occasion in the last 60 days, or have wanted add-ons like drinks, dessert, snacks, or groceries after choosing food.
5. Be comfortable discussing app experiences in a group setting.

Balance the group across:

1. Uber Eats-heavy users.
2. Mr D-heavy users.
3. Users of both apps.
4. Deal-sensitive users.
5. High-frequency convenience users.
6. Users who have had at least one failed, late, missing-item, or refund issue.

## Stakeholder Roles

Invite observers from product, design, operations, support, courier/dispatch, merchant success, engineering, marketing, and business.

Observers should not ask questions during the main discussion. They should capture notes in the synthesis template and ask follow-ups only during the final 10 minutes if the moderator invites them.

## Session Structure

1. Welcome and consent: 5 minutes.
2. Warm-up and last-order stories: 10 minutes.
3. Journey mapping: 20 minutes.
4. Compromise and add-on moments: 15 minutes.
5. Feature wish-listing: 15 minutes.
6. Concept card reaction: 15 minutes.
7. Prioritization and switching triggers: 8 minutes.
8. Close: 2 minutes.

## Concepts To Test

Use the concept cards only after the open discussion.

1. Complete your meal: add drinks, dessert, sides, snacks, or essentials from another same-hub store.
2. Everyone gets what they want: group ordering where each person can choose from different stores.
3. One basket across stores: one checkout and payment for selected same-hub stores.
4. Protected delivery: stronger proof, clearer support, and per-store issue handling.

## Success Criteria

The workshop validates further prototyping if:

1. At least 5 participants independently mention separate orders, group compromise, forgotten items, add-ons, or delivery-fee duplication before concepts are shown.
2. Participants explain a multi-store or meal-completion benefit in their own words.
3. Participants identify clear use cases and clear risk conditions.
4. Meal-completion or multi-store concepts rank near or above core trust improvements.
5. The team can choose a v1 direction after synthesis.

## Decision Outcomes

After synthesis, choose one:

1. Build now: multi-store or meal completion becomes a v1 differentiator.
2. Prototype further: demand exists but the shape is unclear.
3. Narrow scope: launch only add-ons, group ordering, or same-hub two-store checkout.
4. Deprioritize: customer value is weaker than trust, ETA, refund, or fee clarity improvements.
